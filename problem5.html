Dancing drop

Problem Formulation:

When a liquid drop is placed on a surface significantly hotter than the liquid’s boiling point, it can persist for an anomalously long time instead of evaporating instantly. This phenomenon is known as the Leidenfrost effect. Investigate the lifetime (the time until complete evaporation) of such a drop as a function of various physical and chemical properties. Explore how this process is affected by the properties of the liquid, the characteristics and geometry of the heated surface, and the initial parameters of the drop.

Research & Results

If you drop water onto a slightly heated pan, it will quickly boil and disappear. However, if the surface is heated significantly above the boiling point of the liquid, something amazing happens: the droplet does not evaporate instantly, but begins to slide across it, existing for an anomalously long time. This phenomenon is known as the Leidenfrost effect, named after Johann Gottlob Leidenfrost, who placed droplets of water into a red-hot spoon and determined how long they would remain in it barely evaporating. Let's examine this process in detail. Observations of droplet behavior on a heated surface show that when a water droplet falls onto a red-hot surface, a characteristic click occurs. When droplets are small, they take on a spherical shape under the action of surface tension; however, if the size of the droplets is increased, they acquire the shape of a disk with a depression in the center. Droplets move across the surface of the plate on a vapor cushion under the action of a reactive force. When colliding with each other, the droplets can merge into a single drop or bounce off elastically. During rotation, complex oscillations occur, which consist of stretching and compressing. The droplet stretches longitudinally and contracts transversely, and then stretches transversely and contracts longitudinally. Such oscillations happen rapidly and appear to the human eye as the shape of an eight-pointed star. The dimensions of the droplet are limited. Upon reaching a large mass, the gap between the plate and the droplet decreases. Vapor does not have enough time to escape entirely from beneath the droplet and accumulates at its center. Consequently, a vapor bubble grows in the center of the droplet, which subsequently bursts and tears the droplet into pieces. The shape of the droplets can vary. The phenomenon of droplets hovering on a heated surface is complex and contains many effects (acoustic, mechanical, molecular) worthy of separate investigation. Regarding the mechanism of vapor generation and heat transfer, the heat flow is governed by Fourier's Law, where a portion of thermal energy passes from the hot plate to the droplet over a tiny time interval, depending on the contact area, the overheating of the substrate, and the thickness of the vapor gap. The mass expenditure of the droplet occurs because energy is spent on the phase transition (evaporation) based on the specific latent heat of vaporization and the evaporated mass of the liquid. The system is analogous to a thermal insulator, where instead of a laboratory material, the barrier is the liquid's own vapor. A thin vapor layer replaces the solid "liquid-solid" contact and serves as an ideal insulator. At its core lies the intensive evaporation of the lower boundary of the droplet. The surface serves as a heat source, and the vapor acts as a conducting medium (but one with extremely low thermal conductivity). Several characteristics influence the lifespan of a droplet. First are the chemical properties of the liquid: if a liquid has a high specific latent heat of vaporization, it requires a massive amount of heat, and the droplet will live for a long time. Surface tension is responsible for the shape: if it is weak, the droplet will spread out, increasing the contact area, and burn away faster. Plate parameters also matter: the greater the overheating, the higher the heat flow, and the shorter the droplet lives. Geometry plays a role as well: if the droplet is tiny, it forms a perfect sphere protected by vapor on all sides. If the droplet is large, gravity compresses it into a pancake, the interaction area increases, and the lifespan drops. To study the Leidenfrost effect on a heated surface, a hypothesis was formulated stating that the temperature at which the effect begins depends on the boiling point of the liquids. Using a syringe, droplets of equal size were deposited onto a heated surface, and the time required for the complete evaporation of the droplet was measured. The liquids tested included tap water and vinegar. When sunflower oil was tested, the film boiling effect was not detected; the oil droplet began to ignite before reaching the film boiling effect. The experiment revealed that the temperature points for the onset of film boiling differed for various liquids: the higher the boiling point of the liquid, the higher the temperature at which the Leidenfrost effect begins to manifest. Furthermore, the lifespan of a droplet initially increases as the temperature rises above the point where the effect manifests, but with severe overheating of the plate, the lifespan of the droplet begins to decrease again. Thus, for each liquid, there exists a specific temperature known as the Leidenfrost point.
